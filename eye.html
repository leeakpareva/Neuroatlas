OptosAtlas

An interactive 3D eye you can explore, take apart and watch react to light — and part of a personal health simulator that can replay your own vision data on the model.

What it is

A real, anatomically segmented human eye you can rotate, explode and study. The cornea, iris, lens, the chambers of the eyeball and all six muscles that move it are individually labelled with their name and function, and you can simulate how the eye responds to light, focus and gaze.

How to use it

  • Select — click any part (or pick from the list) to see its name, system and function, with a coloured leader-line callout.
  • Explore — use the explode slider to pull the eye apart, X-ray to fade the globe and reveal the iris & lens inside, and toggle axes/grid.
  • States — choose a pupil/focus state (bright light, darkness, near focus) or an eye movement (look left/right/up/down, reading); involved parts flash and the eyeball turns to follow your gaze.
  • Telemetry — watch live pupillometry, per-system activity and eye-muscle tone update in real time, with pupil diameter, light level and focus.
  • NAVADA AI Doc — ask anything; it knows what you've selected and teaches you more.

Where this is heading

NAVADA NeuroAtlas is growing into a personal health simulator. Vision data is increasingly captured by eye-tracking glasses, smartphone pupil tests and clinic instruments — so your own readings could be loaded and replayed here, for example to follow how your focus or pupil response changes over time.

The goal is a clear visual companion you can bring to appointments to talk through your eye health with optometrists and other healthcare professionals. It sits alongside the brain, heart and lungs in the wider NAVADA Atlas, all running on the same engine so new organs can be added as needed.

Data & technology

  • Anatomy — Z-Anatomy (CC BY-SA 4.0) / BodyParts3D, 12 curated ocular structures
  • 3D engine — Three.js / WebGL
  • NAVADA AI Doc — NVIDIA NIM · Llama 3.3 70B
  • Your data — eye-tracking glasses, smartphone pupil tests, clinic instruments

An educational and visualisation aid to support conversations with healthcare professionals — not a medical device and not for diagnosis. Pupillometry and muscle tone are scientifically-informed dramatisations shown until your own data is loaded.
